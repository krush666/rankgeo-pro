What Changed: AI Stole the SERP

The old game was simple: rank higher, get more clicks, brag in monthly reports.[5]

The new game is rude: AI overviews and answer boxes sit on top of your hard-earned rankings and politely intercept your traffic before users ever see your site.[2][3]

SEOs have been ringing the alarm that clicks are declining while AI overviews take more real estate, even as Google insists it's all fine and traffic is actually up—meanwhile, actual businesses are watching their analytics say otherwise.[3][5]

At the same time, answer engines like Perplexity position themselves explicitly as "answer engines," not search engines, and happily give users full answers plus citations without requiring a traditional click-first experience.[4][1]

GEO, AI SEO, AI Search Optimization – What Are We Even Calling This?

Fun fact: even SEOs can't agree on what to call this thing we're all doing now.[6]

A 2025 survey found that about 36% of people just call it "AI search optimization," 27% still call it SEO "but for AI platforms," and around 18% actually use the term Generative Engine Optimization (GEO).[6]

Meanwhile, the GEO market itself is already a real industry, estimated at around 762.5 million dollars in 2024 with a projected 30.1% compound annual growth rate, driven by businesses trying to stay visible in AI-driven search and recommendation systems.[7]

So yes—this isn't just some gimmicky Twitter thread concept; there is a very real, very fast-growing industry emerging around getting cited by AI systems like ChatGPT, Gemini, and Perplexity.[4][7]

Why This Actually Matters for Your Business

When someone asks an AI, "Who should I hire for this?" or "What's the go-to brand for this problem?", they aren't scrolling— they're expecting a short list of trusted names, and ideally, your brand is on that list every single time.[1]

Answer engines compress entire markets into a handful of recommendations, which means the visibility gap between "cited often" and "never mentioned" is getting wider than the gap between position 3 and position 15 ever was in classic SEO.[8][1]

Businesses that start optimizing for AI citations now are effectively front-running a trend where generative AI is projected to keep growing alongside a broader user shift toward zero-click and low-click experiences.[7][4]

The short version: being the recommended answer in AI is becoming as important as ranking on page one, and in some verticals, it's already more valuable.[1][3]

What the Data Says (Real Stats, Not Vibes)

Let's talk numbers, because "AI is the future" is a nice slogan but a terrible strategy document.[5]

One market analysis puts the generative engine optimization space at roughly 762.5 million dollars in 2024, with a projected 30.1% annual growth rate as more businesses invest in AI-friendly site structures, semantic relevance, and citation-based visibility.[7]

Research into answer engines has found that citation behavior varies wildly by platform: one analysis showed Perplexity citing external sources in around 97% of responses, Google's AI overviews in roughly a third of responses, and ChatGPT in about 16%.[9]

Another study reported that approximately 24% of ChatGPT answers are generated without fetching any fresh online content, while Gemini responses lacked clickable citations in over 90% of cases, which means you are fighting for limited citation slots in a shrinking clickable space.[9]

On top of that, data from SEO tooling showed that long-form articles above roughly 2,900 words were about 59% more likely to be cited in ChatGPT responses, and nearly 69% of websites are now receiving at least some measurable AI traffic.[9]

And yes, while many SEOs say AI search still drives less than 5% of revenue for now, clients and leadership are already pushing for strategies, dashboards, and budget allocation to "show up in AI."[6]

How AI Answer Engines Actually Pick Winners

Under the hood, when you ask something like "Who's the best [service] near me?" these systems go through a pretty consistent sequence: interpret your intent, retrieve information, score it, then synthesize and cite.[1]

They analyze the question, pull data from a mix of training data and live web results, evaluate sources for authority, relevance, recency, and factual accuracy, then generate an answer and attach a curated set of citations.[1]

Studies of ChatGPT and Perplexity show that high-traffic, authoritative domains tend to receive a disproportionate share of citations, but they also show that fresh, well-structured, and highly specific content can earn mentions even without being the absolute top-ranking page in classic search.[4][1]

Tests on answer engines suggest that for well-optimized content in a focused niche, you can see citation rates in the ballpark of 40–60% for your target queries, while generic content lingers closer to 15–25% or worse.[8]

Platforms like Perplexity are especially recency-focused, often preferring content from the last few days and rewarding strong domain authority, clear editorial structure, and topics that already perform well in tech and marketing circles.[9][1]

What I'm Doing Differently Now (My GEO/AI Playbook)

After watching traditional SEO and AI search collide, I stopped treating AI visibility as a side quest and started building strategies where "get cited by AI" sits right alongside "rank on page one" as a core outcome.[3]

At a structural level, that means focusing heavily on schema, structured data, internal link architecture, and content chunking so AI systems can more easily extract precise answers, entities, and relationships from a site.[6][7]

It also means leaning into digital PR, unlinked brand mentions, and citations in authoritative ecosystems—think high-trust articles, niche communities, and reference-style pages that answer engines already love to quote.[6][1]

On the content side, I build pages that behave like "AI bait": clearly scoped topics, strong headings, embedded statistics, FAQ blocks, and short, self-contained answer sections that models can safely lift into generated responses.[9][1]

Then I track AI mentions across tools like ChatGPT, Perplexity, and Gemini, monitoring when brands start appearing in answers so we can double down on what's working instead of guessing in the dark.[1][6]
